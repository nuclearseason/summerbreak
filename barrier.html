What are the effects of a language barrier on parent-child intimacy?



The subjects of our data are a Korean mother and daughter who live in the United States. The mother is in her early sixties and the daughter in her early twenties. The mother is fluent in Korean and Korean-dominant, while the daughter is not fluent in Korean and instead English-dominant. As we listen to the recorded conversation, occurring primarily in Korean, it is apparent that there is a significant language barrier between the two speakers. The daughter has an Americanized accent and struggles with speaking multi-syllable phrases in a consistent tone and volume, and without multiple pauses, elongations, or filler words like 어 ("uh") or 그 (“that” or “the”). In fact, much of the daughter’s utterances is a repetition of the same monosyllabic line: 음, which is a kind of casual humming indicating “yes.”

These details reveal an imbalance in the communication between the mother and daughter. The mother is the primary communicator. She offers new conversation prompts and asks questions more frequently. In the transcribed portion of the phone call, there are six clear changes in topic after the beginning of the conversation. The seven conversation topics, including the topic that begins the conversation, are (1) general well-being and weather, (2) tuition, (3) affection towards the daughter, (4) the mother’s hair, (5) a family member’s summer plans, (6) affection towards the daughter, and (7) the daughter’s jobs. Of these seven topics, five are related directly to the daughter, two are related directly to the mother, and one is directly related to neither (the mother and daughter are both directly related to the topic of general well-being and weather). Therefore we see that most of the conversation revolves around the daughter—however, all topic changes are prompted by the mother. The daughter does not initiate new conversation, but merely responds to her mother’s prompt of choice.

We find an imbalance in the question-asking methods of the speakers as well. The mother asks the following non-clarifying questions:

  1. …byeol il eob-ji? / Everything is going okay, right?
  2. …i-jae bi an-wa? / It doesn’t rain now?
  3. …nab-bu-geum han beon nam-at-ji? / There’s one more tuition [payment] left, right?
  4. …sae-hak-ki si-jak-ha-gi jeon-kka-ji nae-ya-ji? / It has to be paid before the new quarter starts, right?
  5. …jal meok-go it-ji? / You’re eating well, right?
  6. …il hae-sseo? / Did you work?
  7. …geu-geo hae? / Do you do that [job]?
  8. …myeot nal ga? / Which days do you go?

All of the mother’s questions are directed towards understanding something about the daughter’s life. Note that many of these end in -ji, which is a question marker indicating the request for confirmation of pre-existing knowledge, like the English question marker “…right?” The mother’s question-asking methods aim, in general, to confirm her daughter’s well-being, and the mother’s awareness of it.

The daughter’s questions, however, are very different. She asks the following non-clarifying questions:

  1. …yeom-saek an hae? / You’re not going to dye [your hair]?
  2. …chil-wol? / July?
  3. …wae on-dae? / Why is she coming?
  4. …ol-hae jor-eob hae-sseo? / Did she graduate this year?
  5. …dae-hak-won jor-eob han geo-ya? / Did she graduate from grad school?
  6. …an jor-eob haet-ji? / She wasn’t graduated [yet then], right?

Most of the daughter’s questions are asked to request new knowledge about her cousin (the “she” referred to above), rather than confirm pre-established knowledge about her mother. In fact, only one question is asked about her mother (“You’re not going to dye your hair?”).

The imbalances observed here bring attention to the halted intimacy between the mother and the daughter. The mother pursues intimacy by asking questions that maintain familiarity with her daughter’s life. On the other hand, the daughter avoids intimacy by neglecting to ask questions about her mother. By asking questions mostly only to hear news about extended family members, she signals that she is more interested in learning new information and less in establishing emotional connection with her immediate conversation partner.

Codeswitching is a strategy often used by English-dominant Korean children to lower the language barrier within parent-child communication. For instance, the child will speak to the parent first in Korean, then switch to their more comfortable language, English, when they are not confident in expressing themselves in Korean. In the case of our subjects, the daughter rarely code-switches even though she is clearly more comfortable in English. In fact, her refusal to code-switch seems to be what leads to her limited range of responses that largely consists of the repeated 음—if she chose to code-switch to English, she would have the range to express herself with more emotion. She says this 음 mostly in monotone, which is expected as it is only one syllable, but the monotone voice and the repetition creates the effect of distance between the two speakers. In this parent-child relationship, the language barrier is still a tangible obstacle, especially because the daughter does not actively attempt to lower it. This conversation indicates that the severity of the language barrier negatively affects the mother and daughter’s ability to be fully intimate with each other as parent and child.

Despite the daughter’s lack of effort to lower the language barrier, she is not totally inexpressive. The daughter actually uses a strategy for conveying meaning past her limited speaking ability. Though her voice is monotone, by lengthening her 음 or raising her tone in different contexts, she alters the nuance of the repeated noise, so that while sometimes her 음 means “yes,” other times her 음 might mean “yes?”, oh,” or “umm/hmm.” For example, see this piece of dialogue:

엄마: 어제 우리는 비 많이—좀 많이 왔고
Mom: here it rained quite a bit yesterday and

Daughter: yeah

In the first instance of 음 (1), the daughter says it to mean something like “yeah, I see,” as the mother did not fully complete her thought in the line before. The lengthening of the 음 here suggests she says “yeah, I see” rather than “yeah, continue,” as the latter would likely be conveyed through a more blunt utterance. In (2), 음 changes to signal agreement, as in “yeah, here too.” In (3), the raising at the end of the 음 signals that it means “hm?” or “yes?” in response to the mother calling the daughter’s name. This strategy reveals that although the language barrier severely hinders parent-child intimacy, intimacy is not necessarily absent from their relationship.
